## Primary Research Question

How shall Morning Star govern constitutional verification within the ORYNTH ecosystem while confirming integrity, continuity, admissibility, and reconstruction without altering constitutional identity, framework independence, provenance, semantic integrity, dependency ordering, evidence integrity, or authority boundaries?

---

## Secondary Questions

1. What constitutes constitutional verification?
2. How does verification differ from evaluation, application, comprehension, learning, discovery, and navigation?
3. What conditions establish constitutional integrity?
4. How shall verification confirm continuity across execution layers?
5. How shall verification preserve provenance and evidence chains?
6. How shall verification states remain reconstructable?
7. What conditions render constitutional verification incomplete or inadmissible?

---

## Governing Hypothesis

A constitutionally governed verification architecture can confirm integrity and continuity when verification preserves:

- constitutional identity;
- framework independence;
- dependency ordering;
- provenance;
- evidence integrity;
- semantic integrity;
- deterministic reconstruction.

---

## Null Condition

The verification architecture fails constitutionally if it:

- verifies without evidence;
- confirms states without provenance;
- confuses verification with authority;
- collapses framework boundaries;
- bypasses dependencies;
- modifies constitutional meaning;
- produces unreconstructable verification states.

---

## Constitutional Constraint

Morning Star governs constitutional verification.

It may confirm integrity, continuity, and admissibility.

It shall not redefine, replace, supersede, or modify the constitutional authority, mathematics, evidence, governance, scientific conclusions, or evolution of any admitted ORYNTH framework.
