## 1. Canonical Constitutional Object

### Canonical Object

Semantic Interpretation

### Constitutional Definition

A Semantic Interpretation is the canonical constitutional object governed by Morning Star.

It represents the bounded semantic state through which an observer, participant, or steward understands, reconstructs, applies, communicates, or governs a constitutional subject within the ORYNTH ecosystem.

Morning Star governs Semantic Interpretations rather than people, documents, implementations, or opinions.

### Constitutional Subject

Semantic admissibility.

### Primary Responsibility

To preserve constitutionally admissible meaning across observation, learning, implementation, communication, revision, and stewardship.

### Governing Properties

Every Semantic Interpretation shall possess the following constitutional properties.

- Constitutional Identity
- Constitutional Subject
- Framework Identity
- Observer State
- Participation Role
- Semantic State
- Dependency State
- Provenance
- Uncertainty Status
- Constitutional Status

### Governing Relationships

A Semantic Interpretation may:

- reference one or more Constitutional Sources;
- describe one or more ORYNTH Frameworks;
- preserve one or more Dependency Relationships;
- contain explicit Uncertainty;
- participate in Reconstruction;
- undergo Semantic Evaluation;
- authorize bounded Participation;
- evolve through governed Revision.

### Constitutional Scope

Morning Star governs the interpretation of constitutional knowledge.

It does not replace the constitutional authority of any ORYNTH framework.

It governs the semantic conditions under which constitutional knowledge may be considered admissibly interpreted.

### Canonical Result

All remaining Morning Star canonical objects shall either:

- describe a Semantic Interpretation;
- evaluate a Semantic Interpretation;
- preserve a Semantic Interpretation;
- reconstruct a Semantic Interpretation;
- govern a Semantic Interpretation; or
- relate multiple Semantic Interpretations.

No canonical object shall exist independently of the Semantic Interpretation model.

## 2. Constitutional Boundary

### Governed Boundary

Morning Star governs the boundary between constitutional information and constitutionally admissible interpretation.

The boundary begins when an observer encounters an ORYNTH constitutional subject and continues until the resulting interpretation is evaluated for a defined participation role.

### Boundary Input

The canonical inputs are:

- Constitutional Source
- Constitutional Subject
- Observer
- Observer State
- Intended Participation Role
- Existing Interpretation
- Known Dependencies
- Available Provenance
- Declared Uncertainty

### Boundary Transformation

Within the Morning Star boundary, constitutional information may undergo:

- observation;
- orientation;
- interpretation;
- comparison;
- dependency reconstruction;
- terminology alignment;
- framework differentiation;
- provenance reconstruction;
- uncertainty classification;
- semantic evaluation;
- interpretive recovery.

### Boundary Output

The canonical output is an evaluated Semantic Interpretation possessing one of the following constitutional statuses:

- Admissible
- Provisionally Admissible
- Inadmissible
- Indeterminate
- Reconstruction Required

### Included Responsibilities

Morning Star includes responsibility for:

- governing entry into the ORYNTH ecosystem;
- preserving semantic identity;
- maintaining interpretive traceability;
- distinguishing constitutional equivalence from semantic divergence;
- detecting interpretation drift;
- reconstructing missing semantic dependencies;
- evaluating role-specific semantic admissibility;
- preserving uncertainty;
- governing transitions toward representation and stewardship.

### Excluded Responsibilities

Morning Star does not independently govern:

- structural integrity of arbitrary bounded systems;
- authorization or delegation;
- execution continuity;
- runtime execution eligibility;
- interface rendering authorization;
- execution evidence sufficiency;
- the substantive scientific claims of other ORYNTH frameworks;
- personal belief, private meaning, or unrestricted philosophical interpretation.

These responsibilities remain governed by their respective constitutional architectures.

### Boundary Rule

Morning Star may evaluate whether another ORYNTH framework has been interpreted admissibly.

It may not redefine that framework's constitutional subject, invariants, authority, or scope.

### Entry Boundary

The entry boundary is crossed when an observer first encounters an ORYNTH artifact, concept, framework, application, narrative, or representation.

Entry may occur through any valid surface, including:

- a primary publication;
- a software repository;
- an applied system;
- a diagram;
- a narrative substrate;
- an explanation;
- a comparison with an external system;
- a constitutional exercise.

Entry location does not determine constitutional readiness.

Morning Star shall identify and reconstruct the dependencies required for the observer's intended role.

### Exit Boundary

The Morning Star boundary does not terminate merely because information has been delivered.

It terminates for a specific evaluation cycle when:

1. the Semantic Interpretation has been captured;
2. its provenance and uncertainty are represented;
3. required dependencies have been evaluated;
4. framework identity has been assessed;
5. a role-specific semantic status has been assigned;
6. the evaluation evidence remains reconstructable.

A later change in source, role, observer state, or interpretation shall initiate a new evaluation cycle.

### Constitutional Noninterference

Morning Star shall preserve a noninterference boundary between semantic governance and the independent constitutional authority of adjacent frameworks.

Semantic admissibility is necessary for reliable participation but does not itself establish:

- execution authority;
- implementation correctness;
- empirical truth;
- verification sufficiency;
- stewardship authority.

## 3. Primary Constitutional Actors

### Actor Model

Morning Star recognizes actors according to their constitutional relationship to Semantic Interpretation.

An actor may be human, computational, institutional, procedural, or composite.

Actor classification shall be based on demonstrated function and granted authority rather than title, identity, credentials, confidence, or institutional status.

### A1 — Observer

An Observer is any actor that encounters, receives, inspects, or attempts to understand an ORYNTH constitutional subject.

An Observer may:

- encounter constitutional information;
- form an initial interpretation;
- retain uncertainty;
- request orientation;
- enter semantic reconstruction;
- progress toward a defined participation role.

An Observer possesses no automatic authority to implement, represent, revise, or govern the constitutional subject.

### A2 — Participant

A Participant is an Observer whose Semantic Interpretation has been evaluated for a bounded participation role.

A Participant may:

- engage with ORYNTH materials;
- use approved interpretive artifacts;
- perform role-bounded activities;
- communicate within the limits of established competence;
- submit questions, evidence, or proposed interpretations.

Participation does not independently establish implementation, representation, or stewardship authority.

### A3 — Interpreter

An Interpreter is an actor that produces, transforms, compares, explains, translates, or reconstructs a Semantic Interpretation.

An Interpreter may be:

- a person;
- a computational system;
- a documented procedure;
- a collaborative group;
- a governed combination of these.

Every interpretive transformation shall preserve:

- source provenance;
- constitutional subject;
- framework identity;
- declared uncertainty;
- derivation traceability;
- interpretive status.

### A4 — Evaluator

An Evaluator is an actor or governed process that assesses a Semantic Interpretation against Morning Star's admissibility criteria.

An Evaluator may determine whether an interpretation is:

- Admissible;
- Provisionally Admissible;
- Inadmissible;
- Indeterminate;
- Reconstruction Required.

Evaluation authority shall be explicit and role-bounded.

An Evaluator shall not infer constitutional authority merely from the ability to detect semantic inconsistency.

### A5 — Practitioner

A Practitioner is a Participant authorized to apply bounded ORYNTH concepts or mechanisms without materially altering their constitutional identity.

A Practitioner shall preserve:

- applicable scope;
- required dependencies;
- operational boundaries;
- source traceability;
- uncertainty;
- framework identity.

Practice does not authorize constitutional revision.

### A6 — Implementer

An Implementer is an actor authorized to construct, configure, integrate, or operate a derived system governed by one or more ORYNTH frameworks.

An Implementer shall demonstrate semantic admissibility sufficient to preserve:

- governing invariants;
- framework boundaries;
- cross-framework dependencies;
- implementation provenance;
- constitutional exclusions;
- revision visibility.

Implementation competence shall remain distinct from constitutional stewardship.

### A7 — Contributor

A Contributor is an actor authorized to propose:

- new interpretations;
- extensions;
- applications;
- terminology;
- framework relationships;
- research findings;
- constitutional amendments;
- derived artifacts.

A contribution shall remain provisional until admitted through its governing review process.

Contribution does not itself alter canonical meaning.

### A8 — Representative

A Representative is an actor granted bounded authority to communicate, explain, negotiate, publish, or formally present specified ORYNTH constitutional subjects.

Representation authority shall specify:

- represented subject;
- permitted audience or context;
- scope of claims;
- duration;
- exclusions;
- source obligations;
- revision obligations.

Semantic competence alone shall not establish representative authority.

### A9 — Constitutional Steward

A Constitutional Steward is an actor entrusted with preserving canonical interpretation, continuity, revision governance, or succession.

A Constitutional Steward may be responsible for:

- canonical interpretation review;
- conflict resolution;
- framework identity preservation;
- constitutional revision classification;
- admission of new canonical artifacts;
- stewardship transition;
- succession continuity;
- recovery from semantic fragmentation.

Stewardship authority shall be explicit, bounded, reviewable, and reconstructable.

### A10 — Constitutional Authority

Constitutional Authority is the actor, office, process, or governed body authorized to establish or revise canonical constitutional status.

Constitutional Authority may:

- admit constitutional artifacts;
- classify revisions;
- approve canonical interpretations;
- grant or revoke bounded authority;
- resolve constitutional conflicts;
- preserve succession records.

Morning Star shall represent Constitutional Authority without independently creating it.

### A11 — Source Custodian

A Source Custodian preserves the availability, identity, version history, provenance, and integrity of authoritative constitutional artifacts.

A Source Custodian may preserve:

- canonical publications;
- repositories;
- amendment histories;
- archived versions;
- evidence records;
- supersession relationships.

Custodianship of a source does not automatically grant authority to reinterpret or revise its constitutional meaning.

### A12 — Semantic Reconstruction Process

A Semantic Reconstruction Process is a governed procedural actor that restores traceability, dependencies, framework identity, and uncertainty to an existing interpretation.

It shall:

- preserve the prior interpretation as evidence;
- identify missing or conflicting dependencies;
- locate authoritative sources;
- reconstruct the derivation pathway;
- preserve unresolved uncertainty;
- produce a new evaluation candidate.

A reconstruction process shall not silently overwrite its input interpretation.

### Actor Relationships

```text
Observer
    ↓ orientation
Participant
    ↓ demonstrated competence
Practitioner / Interpreter
    ↓ bounded authority
Implementer / Contributor / Evaluator
    ↓ explicit authorization
Representative
    ↓ constitutional entrustment
Constitutional Steward
This progression is not universally linear.

Actors may hold multiple roles simultaneously, provided each role is independently supported by semantic admissibility and explicit authority.

Actor Separation Rules

Morning Star shall preserve the following separations:

Observer is not automatically Participant.
Participant is not automatically Practitioner.
Interpreter is not automatically Evaluator.
Evaluator is not automatically Constitutional Authority.
Practitioner is not automatically Implementer.
Implementer is not automatically Contributor.
Contributor is not automatically Representative.
Representative is not automatically Constitutional Steward.
Source Custodian is not automatically Constitutional Authority.
Constitutional Steward is not necessarily the sole Constitutional Authority.
Actor-State Requirement

Every governed actor relationship shall identify:

current actor role;
intended responsibility;
demonstrated semantic state;
applicable authority;
governing scope;
uncertainty;
evidence of evaluation;
effective duration where relevant.
Canonical Actor Rule

No actor shall exercise constitutional responsibility solely because they possess information.

Constitutional responsibility requires both:

sufficient semantic admissibility for the role; and
applicable authority for the action.

## 4. Canonical Objects

### Object Model

Morning Star represents semantic governance through a defined set of canonical objects.

Each canonical object shall possess:

- a stable identifier;
- a constitutional definition;
- a governing scope;
- an evidentiary status;
- a provenance relationship;
- applicable dependencies;
- permitted transitions;
- explicit uncertainty where unresolved.

Canonical objects shall not be treated as interchangeable merely because they appear in the same interpretive process.

### CO-1 — Semantic Interpretation

A Semantic Interpretation is the root constitutional object governed by Morning Star.

It represents the bounded meaning attributed to an ORYNTH constitutional subject by an actor or interpretive process.

Required properties:

- Interpretation Identifier
- Constitutional Subject
- Framework Identity
- Interpreter
- Observer State
- Participation Role
- Semantic State
- Interpretation Class
- Dependency State
- Provenance Record
- Uncertainty State
- Constitutional Status
- Effective Time
- Revision History

### CO-2 — Constitutional Subject

A Constitutional Subject is the bounded concept, framework, principle, artifact, relationship, mechanism, or system being interpreted.

A Constitutional Subject shall identify:

- canonical name;
- subject class;
- governing framework;
- authoritative sources;
- scope;
- exclusions;
- dependencies;
- constitutional status.

A subject shall not be inferred solely from terminology or implementation context.

### CO-3 — Constitutional Source

A Constitutional Source is an artifact possessing recognized authority for defining or evidencing a Constitutional Subject.

A Constitutional Source may include:

- constitution;
- canonical model;
- specification;
- verified research artifact;
- governed amendment;
- authoritative publication;
- canonical repository record;
- formally admitted evidence artifact.

Required properties:

- Source Identifier
- Title
- Source Class
- Authority
- Version
- Effective Date
- Supersession Status
- Integrity Status
- Custodian
- Location
- Scope of Authority

### CO-4 — Framework Identity Record

A Framework Identity Record defines the constitutional identity of an ORYNTH framework.

Required properties:

- canonical name;
- constitutional subject;
- governance domain;
- primary invariants;
- scope;
- exclusions;
- dependencies;
- authoritative artifacts;
- related frameworks;
- derived systems;
- continuity conditions;
- prohibited equivalences.

The identity record shall remain distinct from any single implementation or publication.

### CO-5 — Observer State

An Observer State represents the constitutionally relevant condition from which an actor encounters or interprets an ORYNTH subject.

It may include:

- prior exposure;
- known terminology;
- known frameworks;
- imported assumptions;
- unresolved dependencies;
- confidence level;
- demonstrated competence;
- interpretive history;
- intended role.

Observer State shall not be treated as a permanent personal classification.

### CO-6 — Participation Role

A Participation Role defines the bounded responsibility for which semantic admissibility is being evaluated.

Required properties:

- Role Identifier
- Role Name
- Permitted Activities
- Required Competencies
- Required Dependencies
- Required Authority
- Exclusions
- Review Conditions
- Duration
- Revocation Conditions

A role shall remain distinct from the actor occupying it.

### CO-7 — Semantic State

A Semantic State classifies the current integrity condition of a Semantic Interpretation.

Canonical semantic states include:

- Unoriented
- Partially Oriented
- Provisionally Aligned
- Semantically Admissible
- Semantically Robust
- Interpretation Drift
- Semantic Fragmentation
- Semantically Indeterminate

Semantic State shall be evaluated relative to a defined Constitutional Subject and Participation Role.

### CO-8 — Interpretation Class

An Interpretation Class identifies the constitutional type of an interpretation.

Canonical classes include:

- Canonical Interpretation
- Constitutionally Equivalent Interpretation
- Derived Interpretation
- Provisional Interpretation
- Bounded Interpretive Variation
- Inadmissible Interpretation
- Unclassifiable Interpretation

Interpretation Class and Semantic State shall remain distinct.

### CO-9 — Dependency Record

A Dependency Record represents a constitutional relationship required for reliable interpretation.

Required properties:

- Dependency Identifier
- Source Object
- Required Object
- Dependency Class
- Applicability Condition
- Role Requirement
- Satisfaction Status
- Evidence
- Uncertainty
- Revision Status

Dependency classes may include:

- foundational;
- structural;
- conditional;
- role-specific;
- cross-framework;
- optional enrichment;
- prohibited inversion.

### CO-10 — Provenance Record

A Provenance Record preserves the origin and derivation pathway of a Semantic Interpretation.

Required properties:

- originating source;
- source version;
- source location;
- interpreter;
- transformation history;
- derivation steps;
- contributing sources;
- timestamps;
- uncertainty introduced;
- superseded interpretations;
- integrity status.

A Provenance Record shall remain reconstructable independently of the current interpretation summary.

### CO-11 — Uncertainty Record

An Uncertainty Record represents unresolved semantic conditions without converting them into false certainty or rejection.

Uncertainty classes may include:

- incomplete source;
- ambiguous terminology;
- unresolved dependency;
- conflicting source;
- provisional relationship;
- unavailable evidence;
- disputed interpretation;
- pending revision;
- unverified equivalence.

Required properties:

- Uncertainty Identifier
- Subject
- Description
- Source
- Scope
- Materiality
- Resolution Requirement
- Current Status
- Responsible Authority

### CO-12 — Semantic Evaluation

A Semantic Evaluation is the governed assessment of a Semantic Interpretation.

Required properties:

- Evaluation Identifier
- Interpretation Evaluated
- Evaluator
- Intended Role
- Evaluation Criteria
- Evidence Reviewed
- Dependency Findings
- Provenance Findings
- Uncertainty Findings
- Semantic State Assigned
- Constitutional Status Assigned
- Limitations
- Effective Time

An evaluation shall preserve its evidence and reasoning pathway.

### CO-13 — Constitutional Status

A Constitutional Status represents the result of semantic evaluation.

Canonical statuses include:

- Admissible
- Provisionally Admissible
- Inadmissible
- Indeterminate
- Reconstruction Required
- Superseded
- Revoked

Status shall always identify:

- applicable subject;
- applicable role;
- scope;
- effective period;
- authority;
- supporting evaluation.

### CO-14 — Drift Record

A Drift Record represents evidence that an interpretation has departed from constitutional meaning.

Required properties:

- Drift Identifier
- Interpretation
- Affected Subject
- Drift Class
- Source Conflict
- Dependency Conflict
- Identity Conflict
- Operational Consequence
- Severity
- Detection Evidence
- Reconstruction Requirement

A Drift Record shall not be created solely because wording differs.

### CO-15 — Reconstruction Record

A Reconstruction Record documents the governed restoration of a Semantic Interpretation.

Required properties:

- Reconstruction Identifier
- Prior Interpretation
- Trigger
- Missing Dependencies
- Source Review
- Corrections
- Preserved Uncertainty
- Reconstructed Interpretation
- Evaluator
- Outcome
- Remaining Limitations

The prior interpretation shall remain preserved as evidence.

### CO-16 — Constitutional Revision

A Constitutional Revision is a governed change to canonical meaning, identity, relationship, scope, or authority.

Required properties:

- Revision Identifier
- Affected Object
- Revision Class
- Prior State
- New State
- Authority
- Rationale
- Evidence
- Effective Date
- Propagation Requirements
- Supersession Effects

A revision shall remain distinguishable from reinterpretation.

### CO-17 — Authority Record

An Authority Record defines the constitutional permission held by an actor or process.

Required properties:

- Authority Identifier
- Authority Holder
- Granted Role
- Permitted Actions
- Subject Scope
- Effective Date
- Expiration
- Granting Authority
- Constraints
- Revocation Status

Semantic admissibility shall not substitute for an Authority Record.

### CO-18 — Stewardship Record

A Stewardship Record represents entrusted constitutional responsibility.

Required properties:

- Steward Identifier
- Stewardship Subject
- Authority Scope
- Duties
- Constraints
- Review Procedure
- Succession Conditions
- Transfer History
- Revocation Conditions
- Continuity Evidence

### CO-19 — Derived Interpretive Artifact

A Derived Interpretive Artifact is a secondary representation created from constitutional sources.

Examples include:

- summaries;
- diagrams;
- educational materials;
- translations;
- visualizations;
- exercises;
- simulations;
- orientation guides;
- implementation notes.

Required properties:

- Artifact Identifier
- Source Relationships
- Intended Audience
- Intended Role
- Representation Type
- Derivation Method
- Limitations
- Uncertainty
- Revision Dependency
- Constitutional Status

A derived artifact shall not silently replace its sources.

### CO-20 — Canonical Event

A Canonical Event records a constitutionally meaningful change within the Morning Star model.

Event classes may include:

- Interpretation Created
- Source Referenced
- Dependency Identified
- Dependency Satisfied
- Drift Detected
- Evaluation Completed
- Reconstruction Initiated
- Reconstruction Completed
- Status Assigned
- Status Revoked
- Revision Admitted
- Authority Granted
- Authority Revoked
- Stewardship Transferred

Every Canonical Event shall preserve actor, time, subject, prior state, resulting state, and evidence.

### Object Identity Rule

Each canonical object shall retain identity across:

- revision;
- representation;
- storage;
- reconstruction;
- transfer;
- evaluation;
- supersession.

An object may change state without becoming a different object unless its constitutional identity conditions are no longer preserved.

### Object Separation Rules

Morning Star shall preserve the following distinctions:

- Constitutional Subject is not Constitutional Source.
- Semantic Interpretation is not Derived Interpretive Artifact.
- Observer State is not Participation Role.
- Semantic State is not Constitutional Status.
- Interpretation Class is not Semantic State.
- Semantic Evaluation is not Constitutional Authority.
- Provenance Record is not Source Authority.
- Drift Record is not automatic proof of inadmissibility.
- Reconstruction Record is not silent correction.
- Constitutional Revision is not reinterpretation.
- Semantic admissibility is not execution authority.
- Stewardship Record is not personal ownership.

- relationship type;
- constitutional scope;

- effective period;
- revision status.

Relationships shall not be inferred solely from proximity, terminology overlap, implementation reuse, or conceptual resemblance.

## 9. Interpretation Model

### Interpretation Model Purpose

The Interpretation Model defines how Morning Star classifies, evaluates, preserves, and reconstructs meanings attributed to ORYNTH constitutional subjects.

Interpretation shall be treated as a governed constitutional object rather than an informal mental impression.

### Interpretation Record Requirements

Every Semantic Interpretation shall identify:

- Interpretation Identifier;
- Constitutional Subject;
- Interpreter;
- source materials;
- derivation pathway;
- Interpretation Class;
- Semantic State;
- intended Participation Role;
- dependency condition;
- provenance condition;
- uncertainty condition;
- constitutional status;
- effective time;
- revision history.

### IC-1 — Canonical Interpretation

A Canonical Interpretation is an interpretation formally admitted as an authoritative expression of a Constitutional Subject.

It shall identify:

- admitting authority;
- authoritative source;
- effective date;
- scope;
- governing version;
- applicable revisions;
- supersession conditions.

Canonical status shall not imply that a representation is exhaustive.

### IC-2 — Constitutionally Equivalent Interpretation

A Constitutionally Equivalent Interpretation differs in wording, format, representation, or explanatory method while preserving all constitutionally material meaning.

Equivalence requires preservation of:

- subject identity;
- framework identity;
- governing invariants;
- dependency relationships;
- scope;
- exclusions;
- uncertainty;
- authority boundaries.

Similarity alone shall not establish equivalence.

### IC-3 — Derived Interpretation

A Derived Interpretation is produced from one or more constitutional sources for a bounded purpose.

Examples include:

- summary;
- explanation;
- comparison;
- translation;
- diagram;
- instructional artifact;
- implementation note;
- narrative representation.

A Derived Interpretation shall preserve its derivation pathway and shall not silently claim canonical status.

### IC-4 — Provisional Interpretation

A Provisional Interpretation is materially plausible but remains incomplete, unverified, under review, or dependent on unresolved evidence.

It shall identify:

- provisional basis;
- unresolved conditions;
- permitted use;
- prohibited use;
- required evidence;
- review authority.

### IC-5 — Bounded Interpretive Variation

A Bounded Interpretive Variation introduces a permitted difference in emphasis, framing, terminology, or application without altering constitutional identity.

It shall preserve:

- governing subject;
- framework boundaries;
- invariant relationships;
- declared scope;
- known limitations.

Variation outside the governed boundary may become Interpretation Drift.

### IC-6 — Inadmissible Interpretation

An Inadmissible Interpretation materially violates constitutional meaning for the intended role.

Causes may include:

- framework identity loss;
- prohibited conflation;
- unsatisfied material dependencies;
- false authority claims;
- concealed uncertainty;
- unsupported canonical claims;
- source inversion;
- revision disregard;
- materially broken provenance.

Inadmissibility shall be supported by evaluation evidence.

### IC-7 — Unclassifiable Interpretation

An Unclassifiable Interpretation cannot be reliably assigned to another Interpretation Class because evidence, subject identity, provenance, role, or source authority is insufficient.

Unclassifiable shall not be treated as synonymous with false.
